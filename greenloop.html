Circular manufacturing / recycling / additive production

GreenLoop.

NOSMO GreenLoop is the ecological manufacturing track: selected automotive plastic parts are recovered, cleaned, processed and turned into recycled filament or pellets for 3D printing, prototypes and future construction-use products.

Discuss GreenLoop
From vehicle plastic waste to reinforced printable material.

The concept focuses on clean, single-polymer automotive plastics first, because controlled feedstock is more realistic than mixed waste.

FeedstockSelected bumpers, trims, covers and other identifiable plastic parts.
OutputRecycled filament, pellets and prototype parts.
Next stepCarbon-fibre reinforced materials and continuous-fibre printing research.
Operating logic

Ecology first. Manufacturing second. Data always.

GreenLoop is not only about recycling plastic. It is about proving a closed-loop manufacturing system where waste source, material type, processing route, print settings, prototype results and quality data can be recorded and reused.

Automotive plastic recovery

Start with parts that are easier to identify and separate instead of chemically awkward mixed waste.

  • PP / PP blends where confirmed
  • PA parts where suitable
  • Clean covers and trims
  • Low-contamination streams

Carbon-fibre reinforcement

The material roadmap includes recycled plastic compounded with chopped carbon fibre, and later continuous carbon fibre embedded during printing.

  • Short-fibre filament
  • Pellet extrusion
  • Continuous fibre print path
  • Load-oriented reinforcement

Prototype products

Early prototypes can prove that recycled material can become useful products rather than low-value waste.

  • Construction accessories
  • Protective products
  • Workshop tooling
  • Marine / float components
Continuous carbon fibre printing
Plastic body. Carbon-fibre skeleton.

The future GreenLoop direction is a printer or robotic print cell that lays recycled thermoplastic while embedding continuous carbon fibre along selected load paths. In simple terms: the plastic gives the shape, the fibre gives directional strength.

6-axis toolpath concept
Printing as a controlled weave.

A 6-axis system could follow curved reinforcement paths around complex geometry, including panels, ribs, float sections and eventually hull-like forms. This should be treated as an R&D direction, not an immediate production claim.

Technical position: GreenLoop should start with controlled recycled filament and small useful products. Continuous carbon fibre and 6-axis hull-path printing should sit as a higher-value R&D roadmap after material testing, print trials and structural validation.
